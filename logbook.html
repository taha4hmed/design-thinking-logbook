Phase One

Identifying the Gap

"How might we improve safety, comfort, and convenience in maintaining personal hygiene for elders who are feeble?" — The Central Problem Statement

A problem is not a complaint. It is a gap — the measurable distance between the current experience and the experience one deserves. The bathroom, a space of privacy and renewal, had quietly transformed into one of the most hazardous environments for aging bodies.

Aging diminishes muscle mass, softens joint resilience, and slows the reflexes that once prevented a stumble from becoming a catastrophe. We set out to understand that deterioration — not from clinical literature alone, but through direct conversation.

Method
Field Interviews
Subjects
6 Individuals
Core Finding
Bathroom Slip Risk
North Star
Independence

Six in-depth interviews surfaced a consistent truth: any minor injury in a bathroom setting carries life-threatening consequences for an elderly person. The goal was never accessibility as a feature — it was safety as an unconditional right.

Phase Two

Ideation & The Pivot

We entered ideation governed by a single principle: quantity before quality. Divergent Thinking — with its demands for Fluency, Flexibility, and Originality — required us to suspend judgment entirely and generate as broadly as possible.

6-3-5 Brainwriting SCAMPER Divergent Thinking Fluency Flexibility Originality

Early sessions explored cognitive interventions — among them, dementia-based quest boards, gamified daily routine trackers designed to re-engage a declining mind through structure and reward. They were imaginative. They were also, ultimately, wrong for this problem.

The best ideas are not the loudest. They are the ones that survive the honest question: does this truly serve the person standing in front of us? — Team reflection, Week 3

Mental interventions introduced their own cognitive load. More critically, they risked requiring the user to release physical support in order to interact. That was a boundary the team would not cross. We pivoted — deliberately and completely — toward a purely physical solution: one that addressed grip, stability, and bodily autonomy without cognitive burden.

Phase Three

The Weekly Chronicle

What follows is the unedited record of five weeks of inquiry, rejection, and eventual breakthrough.

Weeks 1–2 · Foundation

Team Constructors was formally established. Initial research mapped the arc of aging — how the gradual loss of muscle control narrows the margin for safe daily living, specifically within hygiene routines.

Week 3 · The Mentor's Challenge

Our mentor proposed exploring a dementia routine board. After deliberation, the team declined. The reasoning was simple and non-negotiable: any solution that requires the user to let go of physical support to engage with it is not a safety solution. Physical safety had to be unconditional.

Week 4 · The Prototype

First Attempt — Static Rail Extension: A rigid bar attachment was proposed and built. Upon review, the team identified critical risks in its static nature. A static tool fails a dynamic body. It was rejected.

The Breakthrough — Tensile String Prototype (Variation 2): Thick rubber tensile strings were looped through a secure horizontal bar, with each end grouped and tied to a foam-covered handle. When force or body weight is applied, the strings tense against the rail — preventing movement until the tension is released. Multiple loops compound this effect, maintaining grip stability even under sustained load.

Week 5 · Evaluation & Documentation

Final documentation compiled. The prototype — formally named the Support Handle Extension (Tensile Rope Grip variant) — was evaluated across three lenses of design viability.

Technical Breakdown

Final Evaluation

Design Viability Assessment

The product is a rotatable, movable extension that can be held while moving — allowing the user to maintain grip in their preferred position without ever having to let go. Three benefits were identified from field testing: it is reliable and safer to hold, flexible and easier to grip, and requires less tension, strength, and hand movement than a standard fixed rail.

Desirability

High

Do people want it?

Interview subjects and caregivers confirmed strong resonance. The device addresses a daily, felt need without imposing cognitive demand or requiring the user to relinquish grip.

Feasibility

Confirmed

Can it be built?

The prototype was constructed using accessible materials — rubber tensile strings, foam handles, a standard support rail. No proprietary components required.

Viability

Promising

Does it work?

Low material cost, minimal maintenance, and wide retrofit applicability make this scalable for homes and care facilities alike. Tensile resistance holds under sustained load including water exposure.

This device does not restore youth. It restores the quiet confidence of moving through one's own home — without fear. — Closing Statement
